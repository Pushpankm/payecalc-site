Frequently asked questions

What is PAYE and how does the UK PAYE tax calculator work?
PAYE (Pay As You Earn) is the system HMRC uses to collect income tax and National Insurance contributions directly from your employer before you receive your salary. Our UK PAYE tax calculator for 2025/26 uses the official tax bands and NI thresholds to show your exact take-home pay after all deductions.
What are the UK income tax bands for 2025/26?
For England, Wales and Northern Ireland in 2025/26: Personal Allowance (0%) up to £12,570. Basic rate (20%) on income £12,571–£50,270. Higher rate (40%) on income £50,271–£125,140. Additional rate (45%) on income above £125,140. Note: if you earn over £100,000, your personal allowance reduces by £1 for every £2 earned above this, creating an effective 60% marginal rate between £100,000 and £125,140.
How much National Insurance do I pay in 2025/26?
For 2025/26, employees pay 8% National Insurance on earnings between £12,570 and £50,270 per year, and 2% on earnings above £50,270. These rates are unchanged from 2024/25. Employer NI increased to 15% from April 2025, but this does not affect your take-home pay directly.
What is the take-home pay on a £30,000 salary in the UK?
On a £30,000 salary in 2025/26 (England), your estimated take-home pay is approximately £24,300 per year (£2,025/month). You would pay around £3,486 in income tax and £1,394 in National Insurance. Use our calculator above for your exact figures including any pension or student loan deductions.
What is the take-home pay on a £50,000 salary in the UK?
On a £50,000 salary in 2025/26 (England), your estimated take-home pay is approximately £37,200 per year (£3,100/month). You would pay approximately £7,540 in income tax and £3,000 in National Insurance. Enter your exact salary in the calculator for a full personalised breakdown.
Does salary sacrifice reduce my tax and National Insurance?
Yes — pension salary sacrifice reduces your gross pay before tax and NI are calculated. This means you pay less income tax and less National Insurance, which makes it more efficient than a standard pension contribution. Enter your pension percentage in our calculator to see the full impact on your take-home pay.
What is the UK income tax calculator for Scotland?
Scotland has its own income tax bands set by the Scottish Parliament. For 2025/26, Scottish taxpayers pay: 19% starter rate (£12,571–£15,397), 20% basic rate (£15,398–£27,491), 21% intermediate rate (£27,492–£43,662), 42% higher rate (£43,663–£75,000), 45% advanced rate (£75,001–£125,140), and 48% top rate above £125,140. Select "Scotland" in our calculator above.
What does my tax code 1257L mean?
Tax code 1257L is the most common UK tax code and means you have the standard Personal Allowance of £12,570. The number 1257 represents the allowance divided by 10. The letter L means you're entitled to the basic personal allowance. If your tax code is different, it may mean you have additional allowances, benefits in kind, or owe tax from a previous year.

Income tax calculator UK 2025/26

The UK income tax system uses progressive bands. Your first £12,570 is tax-free (your personal allowance). You then pay 20% basic rate on the next £37,700, 40% higher rate between £50,271 and £125,140, and 45% additional rate above that. Our income tax calculator for England, Wales and Northern Ireland handles all of this automatically, including the personal allowance taper for higher earners.

National Insurance calculator 2026

For 2025/26, employees pay 8% Class 1 National Insurance on weekly earnings between £242 and £967 (annual: £12,570–£50,270), and 2% above that. Our National Insurance calculator UK shows your exact NI liability alongside your income tax, so you see your true total tax burden.

Scottish income tax calculator

Scottish taxpayers have six income tax bands instead of three. If you live in Scotland, switch to the Scotland mode in our PAYE calculator Scotland to get the correct figures using the Scottish rate of income tax (SRIT), which differs from England, Wales, and Northern Ireland for earnings above around £28,000.
